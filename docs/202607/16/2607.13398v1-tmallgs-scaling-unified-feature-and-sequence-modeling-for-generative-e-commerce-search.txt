## Abstract 

In large-scale industrial search and ranking systems, Click-Through Rate (CTR) prediction is undergoing a paradigm shift from tradi-tional Deep Learning Recommendation Models (DLRM) toward unified, compute-intensive Transformer architectures. The primary motivation for this transition is to leverage Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU) to achieve predictable performance gains through Scaling Laws. However, existing scaling approaches like OneTrans and Climber, often adopt an all-in-tokenization strategy when directly migrating Large Language Model (LLM) architectures, which ne-glects the unique feature heterogeneity. We propose TmallGS , a high-performance, scalable universal ranking architecture tailored for the Tmall precision ranking domain. TmallGS introduces five core innovations: (1) Hierarchical Distribution-Calibrated Tokenization: To bridge the heterogeneity gap, we propose a coarse-to-fine pipeline combining Field-wise Saliency Reweighting (FSR) and Distribution-Calibrated Projection (DCP) to project diverse features into opti-mized subspaces. (2) Field-Adaptive Gated Transformer Back-bone: We employ Per-Field QKV projections and a noise-adaptive gating mechanism to refine semantic interactions and suppress element-wise noise. (3) Decoupled FiLM Late Fusion: To preserve high-frequency explicit signals, we utilize Feature-wise Linear Mod-ulation (FiLM) to dynamically modulate backbone embeddings with explicit cross-features. (4) Context-Aware Bias Decoupling: Ad-dressing systemic biases beyond position, we incorporate a Context-Aware Bias Net that leverages deep global context to orthogonally decouple bias factors from genuine user intent. ## 1 Introduction 

Ranking stands as the pivotal nexus connecting explicit user intent (Query ) with massive item inventories. For the past decade, the industrial landscape has been dominated by the Deep Learning Recommendation Model (DLRM) paradigm, exemplified by DNN-based architectures like DeepFM[ 11 ] and DCNv2[ 28 ]. These models rely on a dual-track design: massive embedding tables for memo-rization and shallow MLPs for feature interaction [ 5]. While effec-tive in the early deep learning era, DLRMs have recently collided with a formidable industrial compute wall. Due to their inherently memory-bound characteristics, DLRMs suffer from inefficient mem-ory access patterns, leading to critically low Tensor Core utilization (Model FLOPs Utilization, MFU) on modern GPUs. Furthermore,

they exhibit a saturation effect, where scaling parameters yields diminishing marginal returns in performance[33]. Inspired by the transformative Scaling Laws observed in Large Language Models (LLMs) [ 16 ], the industry is undergoing a fun-damental paradigm shift. Recent pioneers have begun exploring compute-intensive architectures anchored by Transformer back-bones [ 27 , 32 ]. By stacking deep Transformer layers, these models not only maximize hardware efficiency[ 7 ] but also unlock superior capabilities for modeling ultra-long user behavior sequences[ 21 , 25 ]. Despite the Transformer’s dominance in general sequence mod-eling, adapting the LLM-style "All-in-One" architecture to high-precision search ranking introduces severe theoretical and practical defects[15, 34, 35]. Unlike Recommendation Systems which tolerate fuzzy discov-ery, Search is strictly governed by Query Constraints. We identify three critical challenges that impede current scaling efforts: (1) 

The Heterogeneity Gap: Search inputs vary significantly, from short queries to long-tail user behaviors[ 10 , 31 ]. Forcing a uni-fied attention mechanism across these diverse modalities triggers Gradient Conflicts. (2) Signal Dilution: Explicit cross-features (e.g., query-item matches) are decisive in search[ 1 ]. Unlike the dense supervision in LLMs, CTR prediction relies on sparse binary feedback, leading to severe gradient vanishing in deep networks[39]. To bridge these gaps, we propose TmallGS, a highly efficient architecture tailored for search scaling. Adhering to the philosophy of "Semantic Divide-and-Conquer, Interaction Decoupling," our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) Hierarchical Distribution-Calibrated Tokenization: We propose a coarse-to-fine tokenization pipeline comprising Field-wise Saliency Reweighting (FSR) and Distribution-Calibrated Pro-jection (DCP). This design projects heterogeneous features into calibrated subspaces, effectively solving the heterogeneity gap at the input level[31]. (2) Field-Adaptive Gated Backbone: We design a specialized Transformer backbone equipped with Per-Field QKV projections and Noise-Adaptive Gating[ 22 ]. This mechanism refines seman-tic interactions by dynamically filtering noise from long behavior sequences[36]. (3) Signal-Preserving Decoupling: We introduce Decoupled FiLM Late Fusion[ 20 ] to resolve the signal dilution problem. By explicitly modeling heavy cross-features via a modulation pathway, we ensure that high-frequency matching constraints are preserved alongside deep semantic abstractions. (4) Context-Aware Bias Net: We design a Context-Aware Bias Net to address systemic biases. Unlike shallow position debiasing, this module leverages the deep global context anchor[ 6] to orthog-onally decouple complex bias factors from semantic relevance[ 30 ], optimizing GAUC directly. (5) Robust Progressive Optimization: We introduce an Error-Aware Progressive Training strategy[ 39 ] to stabilize deep training via adaptive curriculum learning. Coupled with a Two-Stage Warm-up protocol, TmallGS achieves state-of-the-art performance and stability in both offline benchmarks and online A/B testing at Tmall APP Search. 

## 2 Related Work 

Traditional Recommendation Paradigms. For the past decade, DLRMs paradigm has dominated industrial ranking. Pioneering works like Wide&Deep[ 5 ] and DeepFM[ 11 ] utilized shallow MLPs to capture feature interactions, while DCN-V2[ 28 ] introduced ex-plicit vector-wise cross networks. Other feature-interaction mod-els, such as xDeepFM, AutoInt, and FiBiNET, further explore ex-plicit high-order interactions, self-attentive feature interaction, and feature-importance weighting for CTR prediction [ 14 , 17 , 24 ]. To incorporate user history, DIN[ 37 ] and DIEN[ 36 ] proposed Target Attention mechanisms to weigh historical behaviors against candi-date items, while BST [ 4] and DSIN [ 9] explored Transformer and session-based modeling. However, the fundamental bottleneck of DLRM lies in its Memory-Bound nature. These models rely heavily on massive embedding tables for memorization[ 19 ], resulting in sparse, irregular memory access patterns that critically underutilize modern GPU Tensor Cores, which lead to low MFU. Furthermore, DLRMs face a Scaling Ceiling[ 33 ]: simply widening MLPs or deep-ening attention layers yields diminishing marginal returns and fails to efficiently model ultra-long sequences (e.g., lifetime history) due to memory bandwidth constraints[ 21 ]. This "Industrial Com-pute Wall" necessitates a paradigm shift toward compute-intensive architectures that can leverage Scaling Laws. 

Scaling Transformers. Inspired by LLM Scaling Laws, the in-dustry has pivoted towards Unified Transformer Backbones to max-imize compute efficiency[ 32 , 38 ]. OneTrans[ 35 ] utilizes a unified tokenizer for all-layer interaction via a shared Transformer. While theoretically capturing global correlations, tokenizing strong ex-plicit signals (e.g., matching scores) risks Signal Dilution due to the low-pass filtering nature of deep Self-Attention. HyFormer[ 15 ]attempted to mitigate this via asymmetric interaction. OnePiece[ 6]introduced Reasoning Blocks to unify search contexts, but its high computational complexity poses challenges for low-latency indus-trial deployment. Nevertheless, most existing unified architectures lack Architectural Decoupling. By forcing statistical features and semantic tokens into a homogeneous attention stream, these mod-els often suffer from a lack of Inductive Bias required for precise relevance matching[ 1 ] and struggle to balance the gradient conflicts between sparse features and dense semantic representations[29]. 

SID Paradigm GRs. The Generative Retrieval (or SID) paradigm, represented by TIGER[ 23 ], and recently OneRec[ 8], OneSearch[ 2 ][ 3], reframes recommendation as end-to-end ID generation. While promising for recall[ 12 , 18 ], this black-box approach faces fun-damental Controllability Challenges in industrial ranking. Specifi-cally, end-to-end generation bypasses critical Intermediate Strategic Interventions (e.g., inventory filtering, ad insertion) essential for commercial systems. Furthermore, generative models inherently rely on probabilistic sampling, making it difficult to strictly adhere to Hard Query Constraints (e.g., exact attribute matching), leading to potential hallucinations that are unacceptable in high-precision search scenarios.

## 4 Methodology 4.1 Architecture Overview 

We propose TmallGS , a scalable generative search ranking ar-chitecture designed to bridge the Heterogeneity Gap and prevent 

Signal Dilution . As illustrated in Figure 1, TmallGS departs from the monolithic all-in-one tokenization used in LLMs. Instead, it adopts a semantic-interaction decoupling paradigm comprising five synergistic modules: (1) Hierarchical Distribution-Calibrated Tokenization that projects heterogeneous features into optimized subspaces; (2) Field-Adaptive Gated Transformer Backbone 

which employs Per-Field QKV Projections and Noise-Adaptive Gat-ing to refine semantic interactions; (3) Decoupled FiLM Late Fusion to recover high-frequency explicit signals; (4) Context-Aware Bias Net for explicitly modeling and monitoring context biases; (5) Error-Aware Progressive Training to enforce a robust curriculum learning capability.

## 4.3 Unified Heterogeneous Sequence Construction 

To fully leverage the global receptive field of the Transformer[ 27 ,32], we propose a Request-Level Sequence Construction strat-egy. Unlike item-centric paradigms that redundantly encode context for each candidate, we align the sequence strictly with the User-Query Request, constructing a unified input E𝑖𝑛 shared across all candidates[35]. 

Tokenization & Assembly . We define five core semantic com-ponents: (1) Context Bias Anchor ( e𝑏𝑖𝑎𝑠 ) tokenizes the pagina-tion index to aggregate global context[ 30 ]; (2) Context & Query (e𝑐𝑡𝑥 , e𝑞𝑟 𝑦 ) represent user profile and current intent; (3) User His-tory ( H𝑢𝑖ℎ , H𝑢𝑞ℎ ) captures interactions and explicit intent shifts[ 36 ], where query tokens aggregate linguistic features; (4) Candidate (e𝑐𝑎𝑛𝑑 ) is restricted to Light Attributes (e.g., ItemID) to align with history, bypassing heavy cross-attributes. ## 5.1 Experimental Setup 

5.1.1 Dataset. Due to the absence of public large-scale search datasets that contain both ultra-long user behavior sequences and explicit query-item matching signals, standard benchmarks are insufficient for evaluating the long-context modeling and signal preservation capabilities of TmallGS. Consequently, we conduct experiments exclusively on a comprehensive industrial dataset col-lected from the Tmall App Search logs. As summarized in Table 1, the dataset spans a consecutive period of 31 days, comprising over 500 million samples. To fully leverage the Transformer’s[ 27 ] receptive field, we construct user behavior sequences with an average length of 1500 by truncating extended lifetime histories to the most recent interactions. This consistently long context setting covers a significantly broader history than typical industrial settings (usually under 200), posing a substantial challenge for efficient modeling[ 32 ]. Strictly following the temporal order, we utilize the data from the first 30 days for training and the data from the last day for testing. 

5.1.2 Baselines . To rigorously evaluate the performance of TmallGS, we compare it against a comprehensive set of competitive meth-ods, categorized into traditional industrial paradigms and recent scalable architectures. 

Group 1: Traditional Models. We compare against standard DLRM baselines: 

• DNN[ 5]: Uses a standard Embedding-MLP architecture to capture implicit feature interactions. 

• DIN[ 37 ]: Introduces local activation units to capture user interests; combined with RankMixer as our production base-line. 

• DCNv2[ 28 ]: Utilizes low-rank cross layers to efficiently learn bounded-degree explicit feature interactions. 

• APG[ 29 ]: Dynamically generates instance-specific parame-ters via a hyper-network to handle diverse data distributions. 

Group 2: Scalable Architectures. These methods leverage Transformer backbones for scaling: 

• HiFormer[ 10 ]: Efficiently models long user behaviors via a two-stage hierarchical aggregation mechanism. 

• RankMixer[ 38 ]: Replaces self-attention with multi-head token mixing to maximize GPU Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU). 

• HHFT[ 31 ]: Partitions heterogeneous features into semantic blocks with independent projections to prevent interference. 

• HSTU[ 32 ]: Replaces softmax-attention with point-wise ag-gregations to enable high-efficiency large-scale scaling. 

• MTGR[ 12 ]: Reframes CTR prediction as a sequence genera-tion task, though often struggling with ranking precision. 

• OneTrans[ 35 ]: Serializes all context and sequence features into a single token stream for unified causal modeling.

5.1.4 Implementation Details . All models are implemented us-ing a distributed training framework[ 39 ] and trained on a cluster of 16 high-performance Nvidia GPUs. The global batch size is set to 4096. learning rate is set to 2 × 10 −4. Given the distinct convergence properties of sparse embeddings and dense Transformer parame-ters, we employ a Split-Optimizer strategy: For sparse model, we use the Adam optimizer to handle the non-stationary distribution of sparse features, ensuring adaptive learning rates for long-tail IDs; For dense model, we use the AdamW optimizer for the TmallGS backbone and MLP towers. The decoupled weight decay[ 13 ] in AdamW effectively prevents overfitting in deep compute-intensive layers. To ensure a fair comparison in our experiments, particu-larly in ablation studies, we standardize the model capacity: all DNN-based models utilize identical MLP hidden dimensions, and all Transformer-based architectures are configured with the same hidden dimension size.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we presented TmallGS , a scalable generative ranking architecture that successfully bridges the gap between LLM-style modeling and high-precision industrial search. By introducing a semantic-interaction decoupling paradigm—specifically through Hierarchical Distribution-Calibrated Tokenization and Decoupled FiLM Late Fusion—we effectively resolved the challenges of fea-ture heterogeneity and signal dilution that plague standard Trans-former adaptations. Extensive experiments confirm that TmallGS not only breaks the performance ceiling of traditional DLRMs but also adheres to neural scaling laws, allowing for predictable gains via increased compute. Crucially, successful online deployment in Tmall Search, yielding a +1.52% GMV lift and +1.38% UCTCVR lift , validates the commercial viability of compute-intensive back-bones under strict latency constraints. This work marks a definitive paradigm shift from memory-bound to compute-centric ranking in Tmall. For future work, we plan to explore scaling TmallGS to billion-parameter levels and integrating multi-modal generative capabilities to further evolve the next-generation search experience.
